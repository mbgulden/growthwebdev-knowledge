## Phase 15: Automated Sprite Sheet Slicing, Alpha Channel Masking, and Edge Padding Optimization via Computer Vision Swarms

When raw 2D animation arrays or sprite sheets are generated by AI engines, they are often spat out as loose grids with inconsistent spacing, messy alpha transparency masks, and unoptimized boundaries. If you drop these un-sliced sheets straight into an engine, you will run into severe subpixel rendering blur, alpha halo artifacts, and texture bleeding across frames when the engine generates mipmaps. Manual cropping and alignment in an image editor kills your development momentum.

Phase 15 implements an automated Computer Vision Sprite Processing Pipeline inside the agy CLI workspace. Instead of relying on rigid, hardcoded bounding box splits that fail when character sizes shift, we deploy a dual-agent vision swarm: the Alpha Mask Analyst detects pixel clusters and tight bounding boxes (BBox), while the Padding Optimizer calculates subpixel offsets and applies extruded edge padding to eliminate texture bleed during camera scaling.

### Step 15.1: The Computer Vision Sprite Sheet Slicer Engine

The Sprite Processing Engine reads raw asset grids, leverages multimodal vision sub-agents to analyze transparency channels, maps out clean slicing coordinates, and applies a mathematical edge-extrusion algorithm to isolate each animation frame.

Create this core automation file at ./scripts/sprite_sheet_slicer.py:

## Supplemental Stage: The Subpixel Texture Bleed Auditor

To ensure your sliced frames don't generate faint visual lines or leak border pixels into adjacent frames when rendering dynamic camera Zooms or rotations in-game, implement a local script utility to audit coordinate margins.

## Extra Gaps Resolved: The Extruded Border Padding Fix (Fixing Bleed at Mipmaps)

A notorious pitfall when working with sprite sheets in modern 3D graphics hardware environments is Mipmap Bleed. When your game camera zooms out, the engine automatically downsamples your textures into lower-resolution steps. During this downsampling process, the alpha transparent pixels surrounding a sprite get averaged with the color pixels on the outer edge, creating a faint dark or white "halo" boundary lines around your characters.

To resolve this issue without manual post-processing, your pipeline must enforce Extruded Border Padding (Edge Duplication). Instead of leaving empty transparency space around a cropped frame, your optimization script repeats the outermost row of pixels outward by 1 or 2 steps into the padding margin:

Standard Crop:     [Transparent Border] [Sprite Pixel] [Transparent Border]Extruded Padding:  [Sprite Pixel Repeated] [Sprite Pixel] [Sprite Pixel Repeated]

Automating this edge-duplication process within your preprocessing loops guarantees that when the GPU samples lower resolution mipmap steps at a distance, it blends with matching border colors instead of empty alpha values, completely eliminating visual halos or edge lines during runtime camera movements.
